Hard Lessons Learned in Tilapia Farming

Real Stories from Our Aquaculture Journey

Kavutiri, Embu County • Spring-Fed Ponds • Mt. Kenya Waters

Lesson 1

The Silver Blanket Incident

One morning, I woke up to a sight that every fish farmer dreads—a silver blanket of nearly 2,000 dead tilapia floating on the pond. At first, we suspected poisoning or sabotage. However, a thorough investigation revealed that poor water quality was the real culprit.

What Went Wrong:

  • Overstocking: We had stocked 8 fish per square meter, far exceeding the recommended density of 2 fish per square meter
  • Temperature Drop: During a temperature drop in July 2024, lower temperatures reduced oxygen levels
  • Water Quality Crisis: Overcrowding led to increased waste and drastically reduced water quality

Corrective Measures:

  • Strict Monitoring: Continuous monitoring systems for oxygen, ammonia, and nitrites
  • Proper Stocking: Keeping levels within recommended limits (2 fish per square meter)
  • Emergency Protocols: Report to authorities, sample collection, proper disposal
Lesson 2

The Frog Infestation

During the construction of our first pond, we made a critical mistake by allowing grass and vines to grow on the sides of the pond. We also waited a month before introducing fish, which allowed frogs to invade the pond. I remember wondering if we had actually bought fish because all I could see were tadpoles swimming below the surface.

What Went Wrong:

  • Ideal Habitat: Grass and vines created perfect frog breeding conditions
  • Month-Long Wait: Delayed fish introduction allowed frog colonization
  • Feed Competition: 34 frogs consuming fish feed within four months

Corrective Measures:

  • Chlorine Treatment: Treat water for a week before introducing fish
  • Physical Barriers: 1-foot gap from surface to prevent frog escape
  • Depth Management: Ponds over 2 meters deep for proper aeration
Best Practices

Proven Solutions for Success

Through these challenging experiences, we developed a comprehensive approach to pond management that has transformed our tilapia farming operation from crisis to success.

Our Current System:

  • Water Quality Monitoring: Continuous monitoring of oxygen, ammonia, and nitrites
  • Proper Stocking Density: Never exceed 2 fish per square meter
  • Pond Preparation: Chlorine treatment and one-week preparation period
  • Depth Design: Minimum 2-meter depth for proper aeration
  • Barrier Management: 1-foot surface gap to control frog populations
  • Emergency Protocols: Clear procedures for crisis response

Learning Through Experience

Fish farming is a complex venture that requires careful planning, observation, and continuous learning. Every challenge we overcome brings us one step closer to a thriving tilapia farm. These hard lessons have shaped our current success in providing fresh, healthy fish to families across Embu County.
